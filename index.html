Work

Ocean heat is responsible for much of the mass loss we are observing in Antarctica and Greenland. In Antarctica, ocean-induced basal melt—where relatively warm water weakens ice-shelves from beneath—and iceberg calving, collectively account for all observed mass loss.

My work focuses on monitoring persistent sensible-heat polynyas that occur near ice-shelf fronts using satellite visible and thermal imagery alongside in situ observations. Sensible-heat polynyas are thermodynamically maintained areas of open ocean surrounded by sea ice that recur seasonally at the same location for multiple years. These polynyas often appear directly adjacent to ice-shelves and therefore have the potential to impact ice-shelf stability through heat transfer processes.

The persistence and location of these polynyas can have important implication for both air-sea interaction and ice-ocean processes. By exposing extensive ocean surfaces to the atmosphere, persistent polynyas can drive both regional (e.g., ice-front polynyas) and even global (e.g., open-ocean polynyas) heat exchange processes. Furthermore, our ongoing work suggests a potential link between sensible-heat polynya variability and rift initiation and propagation processes at Pine Island Glacier (PIG), through changes in backstress by removal of sea ice and reduced margin buttressing.

Check out my first paper here linking space-borne and field-based polynya observations!

We are currently building a multi-decadal record of polynya variability at PIG from satellite imagery. Check back soon for updates! :)

About

My name is Elena. I study ice-ocean-atmosphere processes and how matter and energy flows between these interacting systems on timescales ranging from hours to decades. I love working with diverse groups of people that bring a wide range of experience and expertise, and share a common goal of discovery.

Earth captivates me—both in study and exploration. My family lived in northern Italy before we moved back to Canada where I spent my formative years. Montreal remains my cherished hometown. Venturing westward, I pursued my undergraduate studies on Vancouver Island. It was there I first used satellites to study Earth processes. The fact that we can use sensors orbiting 700 km above to monitor millimetric-scale deformation of the Earth’s surface completely blew my mind (and still blows my mind). My final semester led me to the east coast of Australia, offering a fresh perspective on Earth science from a new hemisphere, enriching my understanding of global climate phenomena through a different lens.

For my PhD, I journeyed to Colorado, joining Dr. Matt Siegfried's Mines Glaciology Laboratory. The energy and support in this lab are unmatched. Here, I’ve had the opportunity to grow both personally and professionally. Such steep growth. Sometimes it felt way too steep (e.g., learning to code…such fun!!!). And sometimes, not steep enough (e.g., how am I ever going to finish this degree??).

I love the people I work with and I love this work. It’s what I want to do. This is where I want to be. Although geographically, it seems like I’m always trying to move 647737485 km from home.

Currently though I am home, finishing up remotely. I’m excited to see what comes next!!
